Exploring Impressionism

The Dawn of Impressionism

Impressionism was a 19th-century art movement characterized by relatively small, thin, yet visible brush strokes, open composition, emphasis on accurate depiction of light in its changing qualities (often accentuating the effects of the passage of time), ordinary subject matter, inclusion of movement as a crucial element of human perception and experience, and unusual visual angles. Radical for its time, it challenged the traditional academic painting styles prevalent in France.

Artists like Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Edgar Degas, and Camille Pissarro were central to this movement. They often painted outdoors (en plein air) to capture the fleeting effects of sunlight and atmosphere, moving away from strict realism towards a more subjective and immediate representation of their perceptions. Their work often featured landscapes, cityscapes, and scenes of modern life, reflecting the changing social landscape of the era.

The term "Impressionism" itself was coined by art critic Louis Leroy in 1874, initially as a derogatory remark, after viewing Monet's painting "Impression, Sunrise." However, the artists embraced the term, and it became synonymous with their revolutionary approach to art. The movement laid the groundwork for subsequent art movements like Post-Impressionism and Cubism, forever changing the trajectory of Western art.
